What We Invest In

We're typically one of the first checks in, but we'll also go a little bit later if it's a deal we can get excited about. At the end of the day, we have complete discretion on what we invest in, no handcuffs from limited partners. That being said, we focus almost exclusively on B2B software where you can sell a small number of large contracts to customers who never cancel. Here are a few areas that we happen to be interested in at the moment:

Healthcare

Marketing Software

We are performance marketing geeks with a vast network and really get excited about that kind of stuff.

Dev Tools

Spent over a decade building software. Love anything that helps developers.

Vertical Software

We're kind of breaking our own rule here on the whole "large contracts" thing, but having built one of the biggest home services businesses in the country, we understand vertical software really well and are willing to hop on the right opportunity.

Areas of Market Distortion

Why Us

Most investors try just a little too hard to 'add value'. They end up being at best a pain in the ass, and at worst incredibly destructive.

Our goal first and foremost, is to not do that.

Annoying Stuff We Promise Not to Do
• Forward you emails of your competitors' press releases with the caption 'thoughts?'
• Ask stupid questions like "have you considered raising prices?"
• Make unsolicited intros to consultants with the not-so-subtle implication that you need help
• Make unsolicited introductions period
• Send you on stupid wild goosechases like 'refining your brand identity'
• Give information about you to others without asking your permission first
• Act like we know even a fraction of what you do about your industry

What you're doing is hard enough. The last thing you need is an investor who makes it harder.

What's up with the name 'Negative Burn'?

A company we invested in - that was and is doing spectacularly well - sent an investor update after reaching profitability. In the summary metrics, instead of writing "cash flow" or "profit," they wrote "burn" with a negative number. We found it both funny, and emblematic of the lack of fundamental business thinking in startups. So we decided to name our fund after it.

Don't get us wrong—there's absolutely a time and place to burn capital for growth. We're here to swing for the fences. But we prefer investing in founders who know the difference between investing in growth and lighting money on fire. There's a big difference in investing in the future ahead of profits, and hiring the Chainsmokers for your company holiday party.
